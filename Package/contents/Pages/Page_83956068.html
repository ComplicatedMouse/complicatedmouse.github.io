What Is Gamma?

The luminance of an image is called its gamma and can be expressed on a graph as shown in the following figure.

fz
In this graph, the horizontal axis is the original luminance of the image, and the vertical axis is the luminance of the image as it displays on a television screen.

Gamma 2.2 (sRGB)

The solid line on the graph is the curve for a gamma of 2.2, which is the gamma typically used for computer monitors and televisions.
The graph shows that middle tones to darker parts of images display darker than they actually are.

Linear Color Space

In the luminance depicted by the dotted line, the original luminance and the output luminance are completely the same.
This is known as linear color space.

Problems With Gamma as Handled to Date

In conventional production methods, data is created for the gamma 2.2 of TV screens. Designers adjust their data in appropriate ways so it looks better on the TV.

Development proceeds largely without trouble, but the following problems can arise because of gamma.

  • Even with realistic rendering calculations based on physical behavior, the image may not display correctly.
  • Adjusting the light can be difficult. (When you adjust the light for the dark parts, the bright parts can white out.)

Linear Workflow

The way to resolve these problems is to handle the images in the game program as linear-color-space images.
This method is called linear workflow.
